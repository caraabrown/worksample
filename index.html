The Challenge

Oakland's illegal dumping problem: complex, systemic, deeply felt by residents, and resistant to standard policy responses. The facilitation challenge was as much about building shared working conditions across governments as it was about guiding content.

Participants had different government contexts, working styles, and levels of familiarity with design methods. The goal wasn't a good workshop — it was a team that could think differently, and a solution a city could actually use.

The Challenge

Buying home furnishings online means imagining how pieces, colors, and scale work together. Most people can't — and abandon the cart. Tamara Mack had a genuinely novel concept. The business model, experience architecture, and navigation logic were not. The Challenge

"We know we change lives" is not the same as "we can prove value credibly." Nonprofits create real, profound outcomes — confidence, safety, mentorship, academic persistence — but when it's time to ask for funding, the proof system falls apart. Stories describe fragments. Dashboards capture activities, not value. This is a systems problem, not a storytelling problem. Better slides don't fix it.

The Methodology — Four Moves
  • Capture — primary research into participant experience described in their own words — real human signal before institutional filtering
  • Translate — synthesis normalizing across stakeholder perspectives; turning "confidence" and "belonging" into structured evidence
  • Visualize — executive-legible impact pathway the CEO could walk through live, in a grant meeting, without a designer in the room
  • Operationalize — proof system repeatable by the organization independently — a capability, not a deliverable
"The methodology doesn't flatten human outcomes into numbers. It builds a pathway from lived experience to investment-justifiable value — and keeps both visible at once."
Cara A. Brown

System Map — end-to-end view of how the organization's work creates value, surfacing assumptions rather than implying them. Synthesis Framework — repeatable process so every new cohort feeds the same structure. Interactive Evidence Site — live proof library sponsors could open in a grant meeting, no deck required. Case 10
Keyways  ·  Service Design + System Architecture  ·  Opioid Support Access
Shame was the barrier. The service architecture had to remove it — structurally, not symbolically
A stigma-aware access model built to reach people who couldn't afford to be seen — and move them toward care without increasing risk, exposure, or hesitation. Social-first entry, clinical pathway, participant website, service standards, and behavior-driven branding — five interlocking components designed as one system.

The Challenge

Opioid support programs don't fail because of insufficient resources — they fail because the people most in need won't approach them. Shame, fear of being seen, and the social risk of proximity to stigmatized services create a gap no awareness campaign can close. What was needed wasn't promotion. It was a redesigned system of entry.

The Five-Component System
  • Social-First Entry Architecture — campaign optimized for discreet, self-paced engagement; content and cadence calibrated against shame and hesitation
  • Confidential Clinical Protocol — structured pathway for program managers to engage doctors, reducing variability regardless of which clinician was present
  • Isolated Participant Website — structurally separated from adjacent programs so participation could not be inferred through proximity
  • Service Language + Rules of Engagement — codified standards for what staff say and how they build trust during early contact
  • Behavior-Driven Brand System — color theory and symbology optimized for instinctive safety and approachability signals
"For stigmatized services, privacy must be structurally designed — channels, language, and digital separation — not merely promised."
Cara A. Brown

The Challenge

Southwest Airlines was a significant sponsor. Students were traveling to New York, Mexico City, Clark Atlanta — meeting elected officials, attending the Obama Foundation. None of it was visible in a way sponsors could access, share, or use in renewal conversations. Staff were manually assembling photo collections for each reporting cycle. The impact was real but the proof system was extractive — it cost more to maintain than it gave back.

The System
  • Standardized story format — program context, student reflection, photography, engagement proof — so every cohort feeds a scannable, comparable library
  • Sponsor-ready access without staff intermediaries — direct, anytime access to a live proof library for Southwest and other sponsors
  • Publishing flow that inverts the reporting burden — student submissions populate the platform, staff involvement in reporting drops
  • Credibility infrastructure — Obama Foundation participation, congressional visits, civic engagement documented and surfaced as findable, shareable proof
"This is a service design project disguised as a website. The real work was designing the system — the story structure that makes impact repeatable, the access model that removes friction for sponsors."
Cara A. Brown
What Was Removed

Manual photo collection eliminated. Quarterly deck production eliminated. One-off presentation builds eliminated. The platform replaced extraction with self-service — funders get what they need to stay funded; staff get time back.
